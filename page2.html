How Cookies Are Used

Common Uses

  • Authentication: Maintain user login sessions.
  • Personalization: Remember language, theme, or layout preferences.
  • Analytics: Track user behavior to improve performance.
  • Advertising: Track visits and serve relevant ads.

Cookies vs. Sessions

Feature Cookies Sessions
Storage Location Client-side (browser) Server-side
Persistence Can persist for days/months Ends when browser is closed or session expires
Security Less secure (can be accessed via JS unless HttpOnly) More secure (data on server)
Data Capacity ~4KB Limited only by server storage
Use Cases User preferences, analytics, ad tracking Login/authentication, shopping cart

In modern websites, cookies serve several roles. Here's how they're typically used:

  • Session Persistence: Keeps users logged in across pages and visits.
  • User Preferences: Stores language, theme (light/dark mode), and layout choices.
  • Cart Management: Saves items in shopping carts between visits.
  • Analytics: Tracks how users interact with the site to improve performance and content.
  • Advertising: Tailors ads based on browsing behavior and interests.

Managing Cookies

Most browsers let you view, delete, or block cookies. You can:

  • See stored cookies in browser settings
  • Delete cookies manually or automatically
  • Block third-party cookies from advertisers

Privacy Considerations

Third-party cookies used for tracking can be invasive. That’s why laws like the GDPR in Europe and CCPA in California require websites to get user consent before using non-essential cookies.

Pros of tracking cookies:

  • Enable highly relevant and personalized advertising
  • Help businesses optimize websites based on real user behavior
  • Support free content by funding sites through ad revenue

Cons of tracking cookies:

  • May be set without clear user knowledge or consent
  • Allow third parties to collect data across unrelated sites
  • Can feel intrusive or manipulative to the user

Real-World Examples of Cookie Controversy

Several high-profile cases and investigations have highlighted how cookies — especially tracking and advertising cookies — have crossed ethical or legal lines. Below are some examples where cookies were used intrusively and led to lawsuits, fines, or public outcry.

1. Google and the Safari Workaround (2012)

Google was fined $22.5 million by the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) for bypassing privacy settings in Apple’s Safari browser. Although Safari was designed to block third-party cookies, Google implemented a workaround that still placed ad-tracking cookies without users' consent. This was a violation of a prior privacy settlement and marked one of the largest FTC fines at the time.

2. Facebook and Cambridge Analytica (2018)

While not only about cookies, this scandal involved Facebook cookies tracking users even when they were logged out of the site. The resulting misuse of data by political consulting firm Cambridge Analytica led to global investigations and a $5 billion fine from the FTC. It sparked a major debate on surveillance capitalism and cookie-based tracking.

3. GDPR Fines Across Europe

  • Google (France): In 2019, France’s data protection authority (CNIL) fined Google €50 million for failing to provide transparent cookie consent under the GDPR.
  • Amazon (Luxembourg): In 2021, Amazon was fined a record €746 million for violations related to cookie-based advertising without proper user consent.

4. Dark Patterns and Deceptive Consent

In 2023, regulators in multiple EU countries began cracking down on "dark patterns" — deceptive cookie consent popups that made it difficult for users to reject cookies. Some sites were found to deliberately bury the "Reject All" button or pre-check tracking options. These practices led to enforcement actions and stricter design expectations across the EU.

5. Real-Life Impact

Many users report seeing eerily accurate ads shortly after talking about or searching something online. While some of this is coincidence, in many cases, cookies (along with other tracking methods) have been used to build extensive user profiles — sparking concerns about digital surveillance and behavioral targeting.

Best Practices

  • Clear cookies regularly for privacy
  • Use incognito mode to avoid persistent cookies
  • Limit third-party tracking using browser settings or extensions
